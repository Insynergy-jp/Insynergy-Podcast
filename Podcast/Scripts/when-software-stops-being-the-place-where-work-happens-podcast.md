What happens to enterprise software, and to enterprise governance, when software stops being the place where work happens?

That is the central question behind a shift that looked, at first glance, like a market event but is better understood as a structural one. On a Tuesday morning in January, a procurement manager at a mid-sized logistics company opened her dashboard and found that three vendor invoices had already been approved. The amounts were within policy. The vendors were known. The supporting documents matched. An AI agent, integrated across the company’s ERP and expense platform, had completed the matching, validated the terms, cross-referenced budget thresholds, and released payment.

Nothing was broken. Nothing was incorrect. And yet she felt a disquiet that was not about error, but about authorship. She could no longer point to the moment where someone had decided the work should be done at all. That small scene, ordinary on its surface, sits at the center of a larger repricing that public markets began to register a few weeks later.

In early 2026, enterprise software stocks declined sharply and in sync. The movement was striking not because companies had suddenly failed operationally, but because a category of assumptions had begun to fail. For years, enterprise software valuations had relied on recurring revenue, customer lock-in, and expanding seat counts. Those assumptions rested on a deeper expectation: that enterprise workflows would continue to live inside discrete applications, and that those applications would remain the place where work gets done.

The market was not simply punishing software quality. It was questioning whether the software layer would continue to be the locus of execution.

That distinction matters. Feature competition and workflow replacement are not the same thing. Feature competition is familiar. A rival builds a better search function, a cleaner interface, a faster integration. Pressure rises, margins compress, market share shifts, but the category remains intact. Customers still need the tool; they may simply choose a different one.

Workflow replacement is different. It is not a better version of the tool. It is the disappearance of the need to use the tool at all, because the work the tool once facilitated is now accomplished through another path. For enterprise software, that is a far more serious threat, because the economic model has always depended on the persistence of workflows inside the application layer.

Consider how a typical workflow used to unfold. A sales team closes a deal. The contract is signed in a contract management platform. The details are entered into a CRM. Revenue recognition is triggered in the finance system. A project is created in a project management tool. Tasks are assigned. Notifications go out. Status meetings are scheduled. Each step lives in a separate application, each application charges per seat, per month, and each application derives value from being the place where a particular kind of work is performed.

Now introduce an agentic layer. An AI agent, operating across APIs, can parse the contract, update the CRM, trigger revenue recognition, scaffold the project, assign tasks according to historical patterns, and distribute status reports without a human opening each individual application. The work still happens. The data still flows. But execution has moved.

It no longer lives primarily inside the interfaces. It lives in the orchestration layer, in the agent that moves across systems, pulling data from one, pushing actions to another, and resolving dependencies without waiting for someone to navigate between tabs. In early-adopter organizations, this is already operational. The implications go far beyond efficiency.

When execution moves out of applications, applications do not necessarily disappear. Many will persist as systems of record, storing the structured data that agents read from and write to. But they stop being systems of work. They are no longer the places where people spend their time, make decisions, and exercise judgment. They become infrastructure: essential, but increasingly invisible.

That distinction between a system of record and a system of work is not new, but AI agents have made it economically urgent. A system of record can justify a fraction of the price that a system of work commands. The premium in enterprise software has always been attached to attention, to being the environment where humans spend their working hours. When that attention migrates to an orchestration layer, the premium migrates with it.

And here is where most discussions stop too early. They treat the shift as a story about software disruption, as if the only issue were which vendor captures the next wave of value. But the more consequential change is not about interface ownership. It is about judgment.

When work lived inside applications, those applications imposed structure on judgment. The approval workflow in a procurement system was not just a convenience. It was a boundary, a designed constraint that determined who could authorize what, under which conditions, with what visibility. The application did not merely facilitate work. It encoded decisions about where judgment was required, who was accountable for exercising it, and what happened when things went wrong.

Those boundaries were often implicit. They were embedded in role-based access controls, approval chains, notification rules, and audit logs. No one necessarily called this “decision architecture,” but that is what it was doing. It defined where human judgment began and ended.

When execution shifts to an agentic layer, those implicit boundaries do not automatically move with it.

The invoice agent that approved payment on that Tuesday morning was operating within policy. It was technically correct. But the decision boundary — the line separating autonomous execution from human judgment — had shifted without anyone intentionally designing that shift. It had not been discussed in a governance meeting, reviewed by legal, or mapped against the organization’s risk appetite. It had simply happened, as a side effect of a capable system doing what capable systems do: completing the work placed in front of them.

This is the missing layer. Not the technology layer, not the data layer, not the integration layer. The judgment layer. The question of where decisions are made, who is responsible for their consequences, and how those boundaries are designed rather than inherited.

Every organization operates within decision boundaries, whether it acknowledges them or not. These boundaries define the topology of judgment: which decisions are made by individuals, which by teams, which are escalated, which are delegated, and, increasingly, which are executed autonomously by machines. In the pre-agent era, these boundaries were shaped mainly by software design and organizational hierarchy. The CRM determined what a sales representative could discount. The ERP determined what a finance analyst could approve. The project management platform determined what a team lead could prioritize. The boundaries were rigid, sometimes frustratingly so, but they were legible.

AI agents dissolve that legibility. They operate across systems, outside the constraints of any single application’s permission model. They can execute sequences of actions spanning procurement, finance, legal, and operations without pausing at the boundaries that previously separated them. This is not a flaw. It is a feature of the architecture. Agents are designed to reduce friction, to complete workflows end to end, to eliminate the manual handoffs that slow organizations down. And they do this effectively. But in eliminating friction, they also eliminate the pauses, checkpoints, reviews, and approvals that previously served as judgment boundaries.

The result is an organization that is faster, more consistent, and more autonomous, but also one in which the structure of judgment has become opaque. No one designed the new boundaries. They emerged as artifacts of what the technology could do, not as expressions of what the organization intended.

That is why the market repricing of enterprise software stocks should be read as more than a valuation reset. Legacy SaaS economics were not built only on recurring revenue and customer stickiness. They were also built on the assumption that applications would continue to serve as containers for organizational judgment. Every seat license was, implicitly, a license for a human to exercise judgment within a bounded environment. The value of the software was not merely functional. It was structural.

When agents relocate execution outside those environments, that structural value does not automatically transfer to the agent layer. It dissipates. What remains is a gap: an undesigned space where judgment occurs without clear ownership, where responsibility is distributed without clear boundaries, and where accountability defaults to whoever happens to be nearest when something goes wrong.

So the practical implication is straightforward, even if the organizational work is not. Enterprises should stop treating AI deployment as only an efficiency program or an automation program. They need to treat it as a redesign of decision boundaries. That means identifying where judgment currently lives, where it should remain human, where it can be delegated, and where autonomous execution is acceptable under defined controls. It also means making those boundaries visible, governable, and revisable as agentic systems expand.

The future advantage will not belong simply to the organization that adopts the most sophisticated AI agents. It will belong to the one that knows, with precision and intention, where judgment is learned, where it is exercised, and where it is owned. In the age of autonomous execution, the scarcest resource is not intelligence. It is the deliberate design of where intelligence is permitted to act, and where it is not.

That is why this shift does not resolve into a new software platform. It resolves into a discipline. And that is where Insynergy and Decision Design come into view.
