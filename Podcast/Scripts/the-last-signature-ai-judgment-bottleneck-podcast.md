What happens when artificial intelligence speeds up production, but not judgment?

That is the central question behind a quiet anomaly that should matter to anyone responsible for research integrity, operational governance, or executive decision-making. In July 2025, a study published in Science Advances examined more than fifteen million abstracts and found that 13.5 percent of medical and life-science papers published in 2024 showed detectable traces of generative AI involvement. The number itself is neither alarming nor trivial. What matters is the pattern that followed.

Submissions did not slow. Across arXiv, bioRxiv, and medRxiv, combined submissions for 2025 reached approximately 370,000, a 16 percent increase over the previous year. By October 2025, arXiv announced tighter submission policies in an effort to stem the flow of low-quality, AI-assisted manuscripts.

So the puzzle is simple to state, but consequential in its implications: writing papers became faster, yet reviewing them did not.

That asymmetry is where the larger issue begins. It is not only about academic publishing. It is about the signature at the end of a process, the moment when a human being accepts responsibility for a judgment. That signature has not become faster. If anything, it has become heavier.

To see why, it helps to be precise about what generative AI actually accelerates.

It drafts. It summarizes. It structures. It polishes prose. For researchers whose first language is not English, it lowers the barrier to publication. For analysts preparing quarterly reports, it compresses hours of formatting into minutes. For consultants assembling client decks, it can generate a serviceable first pass overnight.

These are real efficiency gains. The productivity effects on production are not imaginary.

But production is only one side of the workflow. Every draft requires review. Every summary must be validated. Every proposal needs approval. Every report demands a sign-off.

And the volume flowing toward those review points has increased far more quickly than the capacity to process it.

In academic publishing, this is already visible in overwhelmed peer reviewers. They are unpaid experts who now must evaluate more manuscripts with no corresponding increase in time or resources. Some have begun using AI tools covertly to triage their workloads, despite policies that prohibit it. Researchers, in turn, have responded by embedding hidden prompts in submissions, invisible to human readers but detectable by AI systems, instructing the models to assign favorable evaluations.

At that point, the integrity of the review process becomes contested terrain. But the deeper issue is not simply cheating. It is that the human judgment required at the end of the pipeline has become the constraint on the entire system.

And this same pattern appears well beyond academia.

Walk through a large organization and you will find the same dynamics almost everywhere. Meeting decks multiply. When creating a presentation took a full day, people were selective about what justified the effort. Now that a polished deck can be generated in twenty minutes, the threshold drops. More decks are created, and more decks require review. The executive who must approve them still has the same twenty-four hours.

Analytical reports proliferate. AI tools can interrogate datasets and produce interpretive summaries at scale. But someone still has to judge whether the analysis is sound, whether the conclusions are warranted, and whether the recommendations align with strategic priorities. That evaluation cannot simply be delegated to the same tools that generated the output.

Proposals accumulate. Legal reviews stack up. Compliance checks multiply. The production side of knowledge work has been turbocharged. The approval side has not.

What looks like efficiency at the point of creation often becomes congestion at the point of commitment.

And that is where the burden falls on the last signer.

In every workflow, there is a moment when someone must commit. A physician signs off on a diagnosis. A manager approves a hire. A director authorizes a budget. An editor accepts a manuscript. A board endorses a strategy.

This is the moment of accountability, where a human being attaches judgment, and often liability, to an outcome. The signature is not ceremonial. It carries weight.

Generative AI has made it easier to produce the artifacts that flow toward these moments. It has not made the moments themselves any lighter.

In fact, it has often made them heavier.

When an analyst produces a report manually, the reviewer has some implicit confidence in the provenance of the work. They know a human being assembled it, checked the data, and considered the framing. When an AI produces the first draft, that confidence weakens. The reviewer must now verify not only whether the conclusions are correct, but whether the underlying facts are real. They must ask whether the model has hallucinated figures, misattributed sources, or confabulated patterns that do not exist.

So the burden shifts downstream. The last signer absorbs the risk that used to be distributed across the production chain.

Faced with this bottleneck, organizations often reach for familiar responses.

Some call for better AI: models that hallucinate less, cite sources more reliably, and flag uncertainty more clearly. These improvements are valuable, but they do not solve the structural problem. Even a perfectly accurate AI still produces more output than before, and someone must still evaluate it.

Others call for guidelines: policies on when AI may be used, disclosure requirements, audit trails. These have their place. But they do not answer the deeper question of why judgment load concentrates where it does. A rule that says a human must review all AI-generated content may formalize the bottleneck, but it does not relieve it.

Still others invoke “human in the loop,” the reassuring idea that as long as a person is somewhere in the process, accountability is preserved. But presence is not judgment. A human who rubber-stamps AI output because the volume is unmanageable is not meaningfully in the loop. They are a formality.

These responses are understandable, but they remain incomplete because they treat the symptom rather than the architecture. Overwhelmed reviewers and congested approval queues are symptoms. The underlying issue is that organizations have optimized for production without designing for judgment.

That is not a technology gap. It is a design gap.

Two concepts help make the gap visible. The first is Decision Boundary, the explicit demarcation of where AI-generated work ends and human accountability begins. In most organizations, that boundary is implicit, informal, and often invisible. An AI drafts; a human checks. But what does “checks” actually mean? Verifying facts? Assessing logic? Confirming alignment with policy? All of these? The ambiguity is not minor. It is structural.

When the boundary is unclear, responsibility diffuses. No one knows precisely what they are signing for. And when something goes wrong, the post hoc search for accountability becomes fraught. “The AI suggested it” is not an acceptable answer to a regulator or a board, but it becomes a tempting deflection when ownership was never clearly assigned.

The second concept is Decision Design, the practice of architecting responsibility before decisions are demanded, not after. This means asking, at the point of deploying any AI capability: What judgments will this create? Who will make them? What will they need in order to make them well? What happens when the volume exceeds their capacity?

Decision Design treats judgment as a resource to be allocated, not as an afterthought to be improvised. It anticipates that faster production will generate more decision points, and it builds the organizational scaffolding to handle them before the queue backs up.

For leaders, the practical implications are direct.

Audit your judgment load before celebrating productivity gains from AI adoption. Map where the human review points are. Who signs off? How many items flow toward them? Has that volume increased? If so, has capacity increased with it?

Make boundaries explicit. For every AI-assisted workflow, specify in writing where the machine’s contribution ends and human accountability begins. Do not rely on implied norms. If a human is expected to verify facts, say so. If they are only expected to check tone, say that instead.

Distinguish presence from judgment. A human in the loop is not the same as a human exercising judgment. If review has become a formality because no one can reasonably evaluate the volume, you do not have oversight. You have theater.

Design for the downstream. When evaluating AI tools, do not ask only what they produce. Ask what they require. Every output that needs review is a claim on someone’s attention. If you cannot name who will evaluate it and confirm that they have capacity, you are not ready to deploy.

And treat judgment as finite. Human attention is not infinitely elastic. Senior leaders and subject-matter experts cannot review ten times more material simply because it was produced ten times faster. If you want to scale AI-assisted production, you must also scale, or deliberately ration, the judgment layer.

The larger conclusion is straightforward. Generative AI has changed what can be produced, how fast, and at what cost. It has not changed what must be judged, by whom, or under what accountability.

Production scales with compute. Judgment scales with human capacity, organizational design, and the willingness to bear responsibility.

Thirteen percent of medical papers may now carry AI’s fingerprints. But every one of them still required a human signature, someone who said, in effect, I stand behind this work.

That signature is not a bottleneck to be optimized away. It is the irreducible core of accountable decision-making.

So the question for executives, CIOs, governance leaders, and researchers is not whether AI can produce more. It will. The question is whether the organization has designed the architecture to absorb what it produces, so that when the moment of commitment arrives, someone is genuinely ready to sign.

That is where Insynergy and Decision Design begin to matter together.
