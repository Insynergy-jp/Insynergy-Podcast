What happens to an organization’s capacity for judgment when AI removes the very work that once formed it?

On a Tuesday morning in a mid-sized financial services firm, that question becomes visible in a small but telling way. A junior analyst opens her laptop and finds that the morning briefing she once prepared by hand has already been generated. It is accurate, well structured, and in the managing director’s inbox before 7 a.m. The analyst is free to move on to higher-value work. That is true. It is also incomplete.

For a long time, organizations have treated junior analytical work as if it were mainly output: a briefing, a synthesis, a first draft, a report. But the deeper function of that work was never only production. It was formation. The analyst who spent ninety minutes compiling market summaries was not simply filling time before becoming “useful.” She was learning, through repetition and correction, what counts as signal and what counts as noise, what is merely interesting and what is decision-relevant, where authority actually sits, and how consequences travel through an organization.

That is the historical logic of apprenticeship. It was not a sentimental inheritance from a slower era. It was structural. Judgment, in the strict sense, is the capacity to act under uncertainty with incomplete information and accept accountability for the outcome. That capacity is not transmitted by instruction alone. It is formed through exposure, through consequence, through repeated encounters with the gap between what seems right and what proves right in practice.

This is why the distinction between skill, intelligence, judgment, authority, and formation matters so much now. Skill is the ability to perform a defined task correctly. Intelligence is the broader capacity for reasoning and synthesis. AI can accelerate both. A junior analyst using AI tools can build domain knowledge quickly. She can also produce high-quality drafts, summaries, and structured reports in a fraction of the time once required.

But judgment is different. Judgment is what operates when the rules are incomplete, the stakes are real, and the decision cannot be specified in advance. Authority is the institutionally recognized right, and the personally internalized willingness, to exercise that judgment on behalf of the organization. Formation is the process by which that judgment and that authority are developed.

This is where the problem begins to sharpen. AI is compressing the entry-level layer of work that historically served as the formation terrain for future leaders. The productivity gains are real. For many firms, they are also irresistible. A first- or second-year analyst can now be outperformed, in many domains, by systems that generate research synthesis, first drafts, data aggregation, and structured reporting more quickly and cheaply. The same is true in legal and consulting work, where documented reductions in time for due diligence preparation and complaint response are already part of the operating reality. In a major US law firm, one complaint response process reportedly fell from sixteen hours of associate time to a matter of minutes.

None of this is speculative. It is rational, at the level of the task and the quarter. If a managing director no longer needs to wait ninety minutes for a briefing, that is a genuine efficiency gain. And if a junior analyst can be redeployed to work AI cannot yet do, or if headcount can be reduced, the economic case becomes even stronger.

But the short-term gain and the long-term loss do not appear on the same balance sheet.

That is the formation gap. It is the gap between the organization’s ability to produce outputs efficiently and its ability to cultivate the people who can bear consequential judgment later. AI can compress skill formation. It can augment intelligence. It cannot replace consequence-based formation. The feedback loop has to close on the individual. They must be the accountable party, repeatedly, in conditions where the outcome matters.

This is why the current transition is more than an efficiency story. It is a structural change in how organizations produce leaders. The junior professional who is tool-fluent but authority-fragile is an increasingly plausible figure: someone who can operate sophisticated AI systems, produce polished outputs, and speak fluently about the domain, but who has not been formed by consequence. They know what a good decision looks like in theory. They may even be able to explain the framework. But when the organization needs someone to carry the weight of decision under uncertainty, the capacity is thinner than it appears.

The reason is simple. In the apprenticeship model, formation happened through proximity to real decisions. The junior analyst was in the room when the briefing she produced was used. She heard the questions it failed to answer. She saw where the managing director stopped reading. She experienced the correction, the frustration, the escalation, the real but bounded consequence. That is how judgment accumulated. Not all at once, and not through instruction alone.

When AI removes that terrain, it also removes the proximate consequence that made formation possible. The work may still be reviewed. The output may still be scored. But the feedback loop is mediated, deferred, and diffused. The junior professional becomes efficient without being shaped in the same way.

This is why the risk is hidden. It does not produce immediate failure. The current senior leaders in most organizations were formed in the older model. Their judgment was built through years of imperfect work, correction, and exposure. That is why they can look at an AI-generated briefing and sense what it misses, or ask the question it should have prompted but did not. But that cohort is aging. In five to ten years, many of these leaders will retire or move on. The pipeline behind them will contain people who entered after the automation of entry-level analytical work, and who therefore developed in AI-augmented environments without the same consequence-based formation.

The governance structures may look intact. The frameworks may be sophisticated. The decision processes may be well documented. But when the organization faces a genuine crisis, a strategic inflection point, or a regulatory challenge that cannot be solved by protocol, it may discover that it does not have a training problem. It has a formation problem.

That is why Decision Design now needs to be understood as more than governance. “Decision Design is a judgment architecture framework proposed by Ryoji Morii, founder of Insynergy Inc., for structuring authority, accountability, and decision boundaries in AI-augmented organizations.”

That formulation remains necessary, but it is not sufficient. Decision Design must also become architecture for formation. It must deliberately create the conditions under which judgment can still be developed even as AI eliminates the old apprenticeship terrain.

Practically, that means three things. First, organizations need exposure pathways: deliberate moments in the operating model where junior professionals are placed in positions of proximate accountability, not as final decision-makers, but as people whose analysis and framing materially influence the outcome. Second, they need consequence visibility, so that junior professionals can trace what happened after a decision they helped shape, and learn from downstream effects rather than from abstract feedback alone. Third, they need a Decision Formation Layer, a structured environment between AI-assisted analysis and senior decision-making where junior professionals are required to exercise judgment, identify limitations, make recommendations under uncertainty, and defend those recommendations with more senior colleagues.

The point is not to preserve inefficiency. The point is to preserve the formation of judgment. That is the practical implication for executives, CIOs, AI governance leaders, and researchers alike: if AI compresses the work that once formed decision-makers, then organizations must redesign the architecture that forms them. Otherwise they will inherit efficiency today and fragility tomorrow.

This is exactly where Insynergy’s work on Decision Design becomes more than a governance conversation. It becomes the basis for building the kind of judgment architecture organizations will need if they want their future leaders to be formed, not merely informed.
