What happens to professional judgment when AI agents absorb the work that used to create it?

If you spent your early career in a professional services firm, you probably know the kind of work that is now being automated. Reconciling data late at night. Rebuilding slide decks because a partner changed one assumption. Running quality checks on deliverables you barely understood at the time. Nobody described that work as a formal education. And yet, inside that repetition, something important happened. You learned to notice when numbers did not feel right before you could fully explain why. You learned to infer a partner’s reasoning by tracing the edits they made. Through a long sequence of small corrections, you absorbed a sense of what good looked like.

The firms did not design that as a learning system. Junior staff did the heavy lifting, and somewhere along the way, they became professionals capable of judgment. The mechanism was invisible. It worked, so few people examined it. Until now.

In 2025, each of the Big 4—Deloitte, PwC, EY, and KPMG—launched agentic AI platforms capable of handling in seconds what junior professionals once spent days producing: document drafts, data reconciliations, quality checks, summaries, and preliminary recommendations. A February 2026 Business Insider report captured the uncomfortable question this raises: how will junior professionals learn the work if they no longer do the work?

The response from firm leaders has been notably candid. KPMG’s head of AI talent acknowledged that developing core skills alongside agentic tools remains an open challenge, and admitted he does not fully know the answer. The CEO of the American Accounting Association called it the profession’s biggest unsolved problem. At Davos 2026, reporters observed that many senior leaders had given surprisingly little thought to how workforce development needs to evolve.

That honesty matters. But it also points to something deeper than a training gap. The real issue is not simply that AI removed learning opportunities. It is that AI exposed something that was never deliberately built.

To be clear, the Big 4 are not ignoring the issue. PwC has emphasized teaching the “why” behind tasks, alongside an intensive AI onboarding program that pairs technical AI skills with human skills such as critical thinking, structured reasoning, and better questioning. KPMG has argued that junior staff should analyze agent outputs and understand how conclusions were reached. EY has suggested that AI support may accelerate development by giving junior professionals earlier exposure to strategic client conversations. Deloitte has emphasized ongoing investment in upskilling.

Each of those responses has a coherent logic. Teaching the “why” builds cognitive foundations. Verifying outputs develops critical thinking. Earlier exposure to strategy broadens perspective. These are all serious attempts to strengthen judgment. But they share a limitation: they operate at the level of improving the quality of judgment, not at the level of defining where judgment is supposed to happen.

That distinction matters. Decision Support, in the sense used here, means improving the inputs, analysis, and verification available to a decision-maker. It is valuable, and necessary. But it is not the same as designing the structure within which judgment lives.

Consider what it means to “teach the why.” In practice, this means explaining the reasoning behind audit procedures, compliance frameworks, or engagement steps. That is valuable knowledge transfer. But understanding why a reconciliation matters is not the same as discovering a discrepancy at midnight and deciding whether it is a rounding error or something to escalate. The first is education. The second is a judgment encounter.

KPMG’s suggestion that junior staff analyze agent outputs is sensible. But analyzing an output requires a basis for comparison: the ability to imagine how the output could be wrong. That capacity usually comes from having done the underlying work, from having seen how data breaks and assumptions distort conclusions. EY’s idea of earlier strategic access is also useful. But strategic judgment rests on a foundation of operational micro-judgments. Without that substrate, a junior professional may be present in the strategic conversation without really participating in it.

The structural question is simple to state, though not easy to answer: where is judgment supposed to live, and who is supposed to exercise it?

In the traditional apprenticeship model, junior professionals encountered judgment opportunities incidentally. Nobody deliberately planned which reconciliation would surface a meaningful discrepancy or which deliverable would force a hard call. But within the volume of repetitive work, those moments came often enough that most professionals developed a working base of judgment over time. This was not a designed system. It was a fortunate accident. Repetitive work happened to contain small decision points: assess, interpret, escalate, or let pass.

AI agents have now absorbed both the repetitive work and the incidental judgment encounters embedded within it. What disappeared was not just task volume, but the invisible scaffolding that had been doing the developmental work all along.

That is why the present debate is larger than talent management. The issue is not that AI disrupted a functioning development system. It is that AI revealed there was no system—only conditions that happened to produce growth.

At Insynergy, we call the design layer Decision Design™. Decision Design is a judgment architecture framework proposed by Ryoji Morii, founder of Insynergy Inc., for structuring authority, accountability, and decision boundaries in AI-augmented organizations.

That definition is important because it clarifies the difference between making decisions, supporting decisions, and designing the place where decisions live. People make decisions constantly. Decision Support improves the inputs and frameworks around those decisions. But Decision Design asks a different question: which judgments belong to which role, what authority each judgment carries, and what responsibility is attached to each decision point?

A Decision Boundary™ defines which judgments belong to which role—the perimeter within which a professional is expected to exercise assessment and bear responsibility for the outcome. In the pre-AI model, junior professionals had small but real boundaries. Is this data accurate? Does this structure hold together? Should this discrepancy be flagged? The questions were narrow, but they were real. A missed anomaly could cascade. A poor deliverable would be caught and corrected. As professionals matured, their boundaries expanded. Mid-level staff owned project-level judgments. Senior leaders owned strategic and risk decisions. The progression made sense because judgment had been tested within those boundaries.

Now consider what happens when AI agents take over the tasks that defined those junior boundaries. The reconciliation, the anomaly detection, the structural proposal—all handled by the agent. What remains for the junior professional is “review.”

On paper, the boundary still exists. But to verify something meaningfully, you need to imagine how it could be wrong. That capacity is built through doing the work yourself. Without that foundation, review can become ritual. The boundary remains formally assigned, but its interior is hollow. In organizational terms, the junior may still be “making a decision,” but the cognitive act of judgment may not actually be occurring.

That is the phenomenon we can call judgment compression: the collapse of a multi-step decision process into a single act of approval. The sign-off survives, but the reasoning behind it does not. A traditional judgment sequence might include examining raw data, identifying a discrepancy, hypothesizing its cause, assessing its significance, and deciding whether to escalate. When an AI agent handles the earlier steps and presents a conclusion, the reviewer is left with approve or question. But questioning effectively depends on having internalized the earlier steps.

This is why the problem is subtle. The process still feels like judgment. The reviewer looks at the output, considers it, approves it. Subjectively, it registers as a decision. But the intermediate reasoning that would make the decision substantive has been bypassed. Over time, organizations can produce professionals who have formally intact review records and progressively thinner judgment beneath them.

So what should leaders do?

First, make decision boundaries explicit by role, not by assumption. In many organizations, junior boundaries exist in culture rather than architecture. Everyone “knows” what a first-year analyst is responsible for, but that knowledge is informal. When the work environment changes, informal boundaries dissolve. The design task is to specify, for each role, what judgments it owns, what authority those judgments carry, and how the organization knows judgment is actually occurring.

Second, preserve or deliberately reconstruct the intermediate steps that build intuition. This does not mean rejecting AI or creating busywork. It means identifying which steps in a judgment sequence are essential for developing evaluative capacity, and ensuring junior professionals still experience them, even when an agent could perform them.

Third, audit where review has become ceremony rather than judgment. In any organization deploying AI agents, there will be processes where human review is formally required but substantively hollow. The task is to decide whether to redesign the boundary so review becomes real, or relocate accountability to where judgment actually resides.

The practical implication is straightforward. This is not primarily a training problem. It is a design problem. If an organization relies on “learning by doing,” on OJT, apprenticeship, or watching and absorbing, it is relying on an unwritten architecture that AI can easily dismantle. Better training matters. Decision Support matters. But without explicit Decision Design, the organization may preserve the appearance of judgment while quietly emptying it of substance.

The Big 4 have made the issue visible because they have articulated it with unusual candor. That candor should be taken seriously, not as weakness, but as evidence of a structural gap becoming audible for the first time. The larger lesson is that when AI changes the work, it also changes the place where judgment develops.

And that is why the most useful conversation now is not only about agentic AI, but about the architecture of decision-making itself. Insynergy’s work begins where that architecture becomes visible, and that is precisely where Decision Design starts to matter.
